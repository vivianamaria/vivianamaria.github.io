Cholesteric Liquid Crystals

Cholesteric liquid crystals have the same orientational order as nematics, but differ from the fact that the molecules are locally oriented in a plane which rotates around a perpendicular direction (called helical axis) that repeats itself within a length called pitch (p). This periodic helical arrangement of the molecules causes unique selective reflection of light.

Properties

Selective reflection of light

Change of color when pressed

Cholesteric-nematic liquid crystals reversibly change color as the temperature changes. One principal advantage of liquid crystals is their ability to map out thermal regions of different temperature.

If their smectic and nematic phases are subjected to changes in temperature, they change their form and their light transmission properties splitting a beam of ordinary light into two polarized components to produce the phenomenon of double refraction. Also, lyotropic liquid crystals change their color when subjected to disturbances, such as stirring or squeezing that change their layers structure. This results in the appearance of the characteristic iridescent colors of these types of liquid crystals.

Applications

This type of liquid crystal finds use in thermometers, egg timers, and other heat sensing devices. Changes in structure can also be accomplished using a magnetic field which make them useful in calculator or other LCD displays.
